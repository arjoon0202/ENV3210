Course Overview

Limnology sits at the intersection of hydrology, aquatic chemistry, ecology, and environmental policy. This course develops your ability to think across those fields simultaneously ‚Äî because you cannot understand a lake or a river by looking at its biology alone, or its chemistry alone, or its physics alone.

A Guyana-first approach. This course prioritises local freshwater systems and tropical examples while incorporating canonical temperate frameworks with explicit translation moments. The field of limnology has a well-documented temperate bias ‚Äî your career will be spent working in tropical systems, and the transferability of temperate frameworks cannot be assumed.

üî¨ Interdisciplinary Integration

Every week connects chemistry, biology, physics, and policy. This is not a sequence of isolated topics ‚Äî it's a system-level perspective where each concept builds on and connects to the others.

üíª Embedded Data Science

A 30-minute data science module follows each lecture, teaching R programming and data analysis using that week's freshwater data. By Week 13, you'll be a data-literate freshwater scientist.

üåç Tropical Frontiers

Research gaps in tropical freshwater science represent extraordinary opportunities. Where temperate frameworks fail to transfer, you'll learn to recognise the gap and frame the research question.

üéØ Interpretation Over Memorisation

You will never be asked to recite a definition. The focus is on interpreting data, evaluating management scenarios, diagnosing system conditions, and explaining ecological mechanisms.

Four Threads

Four themes recur every week and form the connective tissue of the course. You'll encounter them in every lecture, every discussion, and every assessment.

Connectivity

Longitudinal, lateral, vertical, and temporal. Disrupting any dimension cascades through the system.

Scale

Molecular to global. Moving fluently between spatial and temporal scales is a core limnological skill.

Human Impact

No pristine system remains. The Anthropocene is the operating context for all freshwater science.

Tropical Frontiers

The temperate bias in freshwater science is real. The research need is acute, and the systems are at your doorstep.

Data Science Track

"Data Thinking for Freshwater Scientists" ‚Äî a 30-minute applied data science module delivered each week after the core lecture, building R programming skills using that week's freshwater data.

Interactive, browser-based R. The data science modules are delivered as Quarto HTML documents with webR ‚Äî R compiled to WebAssembly that runs directly in your browser. No installation required. You can edit and run code on any device with a modern web browser.

The lake manager tells you: "We cut phosphorus inputs by 50% two years ago, but algal blooms are still just as bad. The phosphorus reduction clearly didn't work."

Based on the data above, which response best evaluates the manager's conclusion?

(a) The manager is correct ‚Äî if phosphorus was reduced and blooms persist, phosphorus is not the limiting nutrient in this lake.
(b) The data shows the deep water has almost no oxygen. Under these conditions, phosphorus bound in bottom sediments can be released back into the water column, fuelling blooms even without external inputs ‚Äî so the manager's conclusion is premature.
(c) The temperature data shows the lake is too warm for the phosphorus reduction to be effective.
(d) Two years is too short to evaluate any nutrient management intervention, regardless of the lake's conditions.

Answer: (b). Notice you didn't need to memorise anything ‚Äî the data table gives you the evidence, and the answer options walk you through the reasoning. You just need to connect the pattern (low DO at depth) to the consequence (sediment phosphorus release) to evaluate the manager's claim. That's the kind of thinking this exam tests.

Every exam question provides the data, scenario, or diagram you need to reason through the answer. You are not expected to memorise numbers, definitions, or terminology ‚Äî you are expected to interpret information, connect patterns, and evaluate claims. The questions are written at the application and analysis levels of Bloom's Taxonomy: they give you a situation you haven't seen before and ask you to work through it using the concepts from the course.

True/false questions follow the same principle ‚Äî they present a statement embedded in a scenario and ask you to judge whether the reasoning is sound, not whether you recall a fact.

Course Policies

How the course operates, what's expected, and where to find support.

Attendance & Participation

This is a synchronous online course delivered via Zoom every Saturday. Regular attendance is expected. If you cannot attend a session, contact the instructor in advance. Recordings may be made available on Moodle, but they are not a substitute for live participation ‚Äî breakout room discussions, chat interactions, and real-time data science exercises are integral to the learning experience.

Academic Integrity

All submitted work must be your own. For data science assignments, you may discuss approaches with classmates, but the code and interpretation you submit must be individually written. Plagiarism, fabrication of data, and misrepresentation of authorship are violations of the University of Guyana's academic integrity policies and will be treated seriously. When in doubt, cite your sources and ask the instructor.

Use of Artificial Intelligence

AI tools (such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, GitHub Copilot, and others) are permitted in this course for assignments ‚Äî particularly for coding assistance in the data science component. AI is a modern tool increasingly used within environmental science and data analysis, and learning to use it effectively is a genuine professional skill.

However, AI is a tool, not a replacement for your own understanding. Its use is permitted only with full honesty, transparency, and critical engagement. Specifically, if you use any AI tool in any submitted work, you must disclose:

1. That you used AI assistance.
2. Which tool and specific model you used (e.g., "Claude Sonnet 4", "ChatGPT-4o", "GitHub Copilot").
3. The specific prompt(s) you provided, or a clear summary of what you asked the AI to do.

Undisclosed use of AI constitutes academic dishonesty and will be treated as such. I will be checking all submitted code for correctness, functionality, and syntactic accuracy ‚Äî AI-generated code that does not work, that you cannot explain, or that you submit without disclosure will not receive credit. The goal is not to catch you out ‚Äî it is to ensure that you are learning. AI can accelerate your learning if you engage with its output critically. It will undermine your learning if you treat it as a shortcut past understanding.

Technology Requirements

You need a device with a modern web browser (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, or Safari), a stable internet connection for Zoom, and access to Moodle. R and RStudio are not required ‚Äî the data science modules run in the browser via webR. If you have R installed locally and prefer to use it, that's fine. A webcam and microphone are expected for Zoom sessions.

Late Submissions

Late work will be penalised at a rate to be confirmed in Week 1. Extensions may be granted for documented circumstances ‚Äî contact the instructor before the deadline. Technical issues (internet outages, device problems) are understandable given the online delivery context and will be handled with flexibility if communicated promptly.

Recommended Textbook

There is no required textbook. All essential material is covered in the lectures, data science modules, and supplementary readings posted on Moodle. For students who want a reference text, Wetzel, R.G. (2001). Limnology: Lake and River Ecosystems, 3rd Edition. Academic Press is the standard reference in the field. It is comprehensive but temperate-focused ‚Äî the lectures provide the tropical context that the textbook lacks.

Accessibility & Accommodations

If you require accommodations for a disability or other circumstance, please contact the instructor as early as possible. Lecture materials (slides, transcripts, data science HTML modules) are designed to be accessible across devices and connectivity levels. All interactive content has static fallbacks.
